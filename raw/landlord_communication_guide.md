## The Golden Rule: Everything in Writing

Written records are the foundation of landlord-tenant dispute resolution. Texas Law Help specifies for repair requests: take photos, "provide written notice to your landlord (certified mail return receipt recommended — if sent certified, only one notice is legally required; otherwise send two)."

The same principle applies to all landlord-tenant interactions: always follow up verbal conversations with an email summary. This creates a timestamped paper trail that protects you legally.

---

## Types of Communication

### Email (Best for most situations)
- Creates a clear paper trail with timestamps
- Appropriate for maintenance requests, lease questions, move-out notice, and any dispute
- Professional tone; use your university email if possible

### Tenant Portal (If available)
- Many large apartment complexes use portals (e.g., RentCafe, AppFolio, Buildium)
- Log requests through the portal AND follow up with email to ensure documentation

### Certified Mail (For formal or legal notices)
- Use for move-out notice, security deposit disputes, or legal complaints
- Provides proof of delivery with signature confirmation
- Available at any U.S. Post Office for about $3–$5
- Texas Law Help: "If sent by certified mail return receipt requested, you only need to send the notice once."

### Phone / In-Person
- Good for urgent issues or building rapport
- ALWAYS follow up with a written summary: "Per our conversation today, you agreed to repair the heater by Friday."

## Tenant Rights in Disputes

Cornell Law LII: When landlords fail to maintain habitability standards, tenants may "withhold rent to pay for repairs directly, sue for damages, or report code violations without fear of retaliation."

Texas Law Help: Landlords cannot retaliate against tenants who submit legitimate repair requests. Retaliation protections last six months and cover eviction, lease termination, and rent increases.

## Email Templates

### Reporting a Problem
**Subject:** Maintenance Request — Unit [Number] — [Brief Description]

"Dear [Name], I am writing to report [describe issue] in my apartment. The issue started on [date]. I have attached photos for reference. Please let me know when maintenance can address this. I am available [availability]. Thank you, [Your Name]"

### Following Up on Unresolved Issue
**Subject:** Follow-Up: Maintenance Request — Unit [Number] — [Issue]

"Dear [Name], I am following up on my written request dated [date] regarding [issue] in Unit [Number]. As of today, the issue has not been resolved. Please provide an update on when repairs will be completed. Thank you."

### Disputing a Charge
**Subject:** Formal Dispute of Charge — Unit [Number]

"Dear [Name], I am writing to formally dispute a charge of $[amount] for [reason]. Based on my move-in inspection report and photographic documentation, this condition was pre-existing. I have attached my move-in photos for reference. Please review and remove this charge. If you would like to discuss, I am available by email or phone."

### Move-Out Notice
**Subject:** Notice of Intent to Vacate — Unit [Number]

"Dear [Name], This letter serves as my [30/60]-day written notice that I intend to vacate Unit [Number] at [address] on [date]. My forwarding address for deposit return is [address]. Please contact me to schedule a joint move-out inspection. Thank you."

## What to Keep on File

- All emails sent and received (create a dedicated folder)
- Copies of all maintenance requests and responses
- Move-in and move-out condition reports (signed copies)
- Rent payment receipts or bank statements
- Your signed lease agreement
- Any notices received from the landlord (rent increases, entry notices, etc.)
