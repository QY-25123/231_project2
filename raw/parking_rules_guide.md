## Types of Parking Arrangements

### Assigned Parking
Tenant is given a specific numbered spot. Parking in another resident's assigned spot is one of the most common towing triggers at apartment complexes. Some buildings charge additional fees for covered or garage parking ($50–$200/month).

### Permit Parking (Residential Permit Programs)
Buildings or neighborhoods issue parking permits — stickers, hang tags, or digital passes — that must be displayed in the vehicle. Vehicles without a valid permit may be ticketed or towed. If you have a car, get your permit on or before move-in day.

City-issued residential parking permits are common in dense urban areas (e.g., Washington D.C., Boston, Chicago, San Francisco). These are separate from any permit your building may issue.

### Visitor Parking
Designated visitor spots typically have time limits (2–24 hours). Some buildings require visitor passes from the management office. Overstaying visitor limits is a frequent towing trigger.

### Street Parking
Many cities restrict street parking. Common restrictions include:
- Street cleaning days (e.g., "No Parking 8am–10am Mon & Thu")
- Overnight parking restrictions
- Residential permit zones (only residents with city permits may park)

Always read posted signs carefully. Fines are typically $50–$150 per violation.

## Common Towing Triggers

Vehicles are commonly towed for:
- Parking in a fire lane (red curb) or blocking a fire hydrant (yellow curb)
- Parking in another resident's assigned spot
- Missing or expired permit
- Blocking a driveway or accessible parking space
- Overstaying visitor parking time limits

---

## If Your Car Is Towed

1. Call building management to find out which towing company they contract with.
2. Contact the towing company to confirm your vehicle's location.
3. Bring cash, driver's license, and vehicle registration to retrieve the vehicle.
4. Retrieval fees: typically $150–$350 plus daily storage fees. Act quickly — storage fees accrue daily.
5. If you believe the tow was unauthorized, contact local law enforcement and document everything in writing.

## Common Lease Parking Rules

Most apartment leases include restrictions such as:
- No vehicle repairs in the parking lot (oil changes, tire changes, etc.)
- No storage of inoperable vehicles
- No vehicle washing in the parking lot
- Boats, RVs, and trailers typically prohibited without special permission

## For International Students with a Vehicle

- **Driver's license:** Your home country license is often valid for the first year in many states; check your specific state's rules. California, for example, requires a California driver's license within 10 days of establishing residency.
- **Vehicle registration:** Register your vehicle in your new state within 30–90 days of arrival (varies by state).
- **Insurance:** U.S. law requires liability insurance. Always keep your insurance card and vehicle registration in the car.

If you don't own a car, ask your building about **bicycle storage** — many complexes have secured bike rooms.

## Questions to Ask Before Signing a Lease

- Is parking included in rent or an additional monthly fee?
- How many spots does my unit come with?
- Is there designated visitor parking?
- What is the guest parking time limit?
- Where do I get my parking permit/pass?
- Are there restrictions on vehicle types (RVs, trucks)?
