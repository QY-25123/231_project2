## What Is Renters Insurance?

Renters' insurance, also called tenants' insurance, is "an insurance policy that provides some of the benefits of homeowners' insurance, but does not include coverage for the dwelling." It protects a tenant's personal belongings and provides liability coverage.

Your landlord's insurance covers the building structure — NOT your personal property inside it.

Many rental properties require tenants to carry renters' insurance as part of the lease agreement. Lease agreements often specify that tenants must provide proof of "HO-4" coverage before lease signing. Requiring insurance "allows landlords and other tenants to recover damages from the tenant's policy if they cause damage to the premises."

---

## What Renters Insurance Covers

### 1. Personal Possessions
Renters insurance protects belongings against "damage from fire, smoke, lightning, vandalism, theft, explosion, windstorm, water and other disasters" specified in the policy. Coverage includes furniture, clothing, electronics, appliances, and household items.

**Not covered by standard policy:** Floods and earthquakes (require supplemental policies).

**Off-premises coverage:** Extends protection to belongings outside the home, though typically limited to 10% of total coverage (e.g., $2,500 on a $25,000 policy). This means belongings stolen from your car or while traveling may be partially covered.

### 2. Liability Protection
Covers lawsuits for bodily injury or property damage caused by you, family members, or pets. Liability limits generally begin around $100,000, with experts recommending at least $300,000.

No-fault medical coverage reimburses injured guests' medical bills ($1,000–$5,000 typically).

### 3. Additional Living Expenses (ALE)
"If your home is destroyed by a disaster that your policy covers and you need to live elsewhere," the policy reimburses the difference between temporary living costs and your normal expenses (hotels, temporary rentals, restaurant meals during repairs).

---

## What Renters Insurance Does NOT Cover

- Floods and earthquakes
- Vehicles (requires separate auto insurance)
- Roommate's belongings (each tenant needs their own policy)
- High-value items above policy limits — jewelry, expensive cameras (may need a "floater" endorsement)
- Damage you intentionally cause

---

## Policy Types

**Actual Cash Value:** Reimburses replacement cost minus depreciation, up to policy limits.

**Replacement Cost:** Covers full replacement cost without depreciation deduction — approximately 10% more expensive but often worthwhile. Recommended for most tenants.

## Cost and How to Save

### Deductibles
Higher deductibles reduce premiums significantly. A $500 deductible is standard; raising it to $1,000 may reduce your premium by up to 25%.

### Discounts Available
- Bundling with auto insurance from the same insurer
- Home security systems, smoke detectors, deadbolt locks
- Non-smoker discounts

Typical cost: **$10–$20/month** for most renters. Policies can start as low as $5/month.

## How to Buy Renters Insurance

1. Obtain at least three price quotes from different insurers
2. Compare direct agents, independent agents, and direct online sellers
3. Evaluate the company's reputation for claims handling
4. Seek peer recommendations

**Key consideration:** "Select a company that answers your questions and handles claims fairly and efficiently."

## Special Coverage Notes for Students

- College students living in **dorms** are typically covered under their parents' homeowners policy.
- Students living **off-campus** typically need their own separate renters insurance policy.
- **Roommates/domestic partners:** Coverage varies by state and insurer; domestic partners must be specifically named on the policy.
- **Valuables:** Standard coverage limits jewelry and expensive items to $1,500; endorsements or "floaters" increase those limits.

## Filing a Claim

1. Report crimes to police; obtain a police report and officer names.
2. Contact your insurer immediately to clarify coverage, deductibles, and processing time.
3. Document losses with photographs, receipts, and itemized lists.
4. Preserve damaged items until an adjuster inspects them.
5. Keep all receipts for temporary relocation expenses.
6. Complete and return claim forms promptly.

If unsatisfied with insurer response, escalate to your state's Department of Insurance.

---

## Flood and Earthquake Coverage

Standard renters insurance does NOT cover floods or earthquakes.
- Flood insurance: available through the National Flood Insurance Program (floodsmart.gov, 888-379-9531)
- Earthquake coverage: available as a separate policy or endorsement in high-risk areas
