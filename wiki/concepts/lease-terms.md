# Lease Terms — Understanding Your Rental Agreement

A **lease** is a legally binding contract between you (the **tenant**) and the property owner (the **landlord**). Signing a lease is one of the most important legal actions you will take as a renter in the U.S. This article explains what a lease contains, what types exist, and what to watch for before you sign.

> 💡 The University of Alabama Birmingham Office of Global Engagement describes a lease as "a legally binding rental agreement with fixed terms (6, 9, 12+ months) requiring careful review."

---

## Types of Leases

### Fixed-Term Tenancy
The most common type for apartments. It has a clear **start date** and **end date** (for example, August 1 to July 31). You and the landlord are both bound by the terms until that date.

- At the end of the term, the lease either expires, renews, or converts to month-to-month
- You cannot simply leave early without potential penalties (see Early Termination below)

### Periodic Tenancy (Month-to-Month)
There is no fixed end date. The lease continues automatically each month until either party gives written notice to end it. Typical notice required: **30 days**.

- More flexible — good if your plans are uncertain
- Rent can be increased with proper notice (usually 30 days)
- Landlord can end tenancy with proper notice as well

### Tenancy at Will
Either the landlord or tenant can end the arrangement at any time with reasonable notice. This is uncommon in formal apartment rentals.

### Tenancy at Sufferance (Holdover Tenancy)
This happens when **you stay in the apartment after your lease ends** without renewing. The landlord can:
- Evict you without prior notice, OR
- Treat you as a month-to-month tenant at a new (often higher) rent

⚠️ **Do not "hold over" without your landlord's written agreement.** It creates significant legal risk.

## Key Clauses in Every Lease

Read every section carefully. Below are the most important elements to understand.

### Rent
- **Amount:** How much you pay per month
- **Due date:** Usually the 1st of each month
- **Grace period:** Many leases give a few days (e.g., 3–5 days) before a late fee applies
- **Late fee:** Often $50–$150 or a percentage of rent
- **Payment method:** Check, online portal, bank transfer — know what is accepted

### Security Deposit
- Usually equal to one month's rent (up to two months in some states)
- Returned after move-out minus any lawful deductions
- See [[concepts/security-deposit]] for full details

### Lease Duration and Renewal
- Note the exact end date
- Check whether the lease **auto-renews** — many leases renew automatically if you do not give written notice in time (often 30–60 days before the end)
- Check how much notice you must give to move out → see [[guides/move-out]]

### Early Termination
- Breaking the lease early can cost you **1–2 months' rent** as a penalty fee, or you may owe rent for every remaining month until the landlord finds a new tenant
- Always ask: *"What will I owe if I must break this lease before the end?"*

### Subletting
- **Subletting** means renting your apartment to another person while you are away
- Most leases **prohibit subletting without written landlord approval**
- Violating this clause can result in eviction

### Entry by Landlord
- Your landlord must give **advance written notice** (typically 24 hours) before entering your unit
- Exceptions: genuine emergencies (flood, fire, gas leak)

### Pet Policy
- Even if you do not have a pet now, note whether pets are allowed and what fees apply
- Pet deposits are often non-refundable

### Guest Limits
- Some leases limit how long a guest can stay (e.g., no more than 14 consecutive days)

### Renters Insurance
- Many leases now **require** you to carry renters insurance as a condition of tenancy
- You may need to show proof before signing → see [[concepts/renters-insurance]]

## Your Rights Under a Lease

| Right | What It Means |
|---|---|
| **Quiet Enjoyment** | You have the right to live in your home without interference from your landlord or others |
| **Exclusive Possession** | Only you (and approved occupants) can use your unit — your landlord cannot enter freely |
| **Notice Before Entry** | Landlord must give proper advance notice before entering |
| **Habitable Conditions** | Landlord must maintain the property in a livable, safe condition |

## For International Students: Documents You May Need

Landlords in the U.S. commonly ask for:

- **Passport** (primary ID)
- **Form I-20** (for F-1 visa students) or **DS-2019** (for J-1 visa students)
- **Form I-94** (proof of legal entry and status)
- **Proof of funds:** Bank statements, scholarship award letter, or financial guarantee letter
- **Guarantor or co-signer:** If you have no U.S. credit history, some landlords require a U.S.-based person to co-sign the lease and accept responsibility if you do not pay

💡 Some landlords accept a larger security deposit (e.g., 2–3 months) in place of a co-signer. It is worth asking.

## Questions to Ask Before You Sign

- What is the exact monthly rent and due date?
- Is there a grace period for late payments?
- Which utilities are included in the rent?
- What are the exact notice requirements to move out?
- Does the lease auto-renew? When must I notify you if I am not renewing?
- What is the early termination policy and fee?
- Is subletting allowed?
- Is renters insurance required?
- Are pets allowed? What are the fees?

⚠️ **Never sign a lease you have not fully read.** If something is unclear, ask the landlord to explain it in writing, or bring the lease to your university's international student office or a tenant rights clinic before signing.
