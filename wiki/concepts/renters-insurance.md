# Renters Insurance — Protecting Your Belongings

**Renters insurance** (also called **tenants' insurance**) is an affordable insurance policy that protects your personal belongings and provides liability coverage while you rent an apartment. It does **not** cover the building itself — that is your landlord's responsibility.

> 💡 Renters insurance typically costs just **$10–$20 per month**. For that price, you get protection for thousands of dollars of belongings.

---

## Do You Need It?

Many lease agreements now **require** renters insurance as a condition of renting. Your lease may call for **HO-4 coverage**, which is the standard renters insurance policy type. You may need to show proof of insurance before you can sign the lease.

Even if your lease does not require it, renters insurance is strongly recommended. Your landlord's building insurance covers **only the structure** — not your laptop, furniture, clothing, or other personal items inside the apartment.

---

## What Renters Insurance Covers

### 1. Personal Belongings
Your belongings are protected if damaged or destroyed by events such as:
- Fire or smoke
- Theft or vandalism
- Lightning
- Windstorm
- Explosion
- Water damage (from burst pipes — **not** floods)

This includes furniture, clothing, electronics, appliances, books, and household items.

**⚠️ Not covered by a standard policy:**
- Floods (requires a separate flood insurance policy — see NFIP at floodsmart.gov)
- Earthquakes (requires a separate policy or endorsement)

**Off-premises coverage:** Your policy may also cover belongings stolen from your car or while you are traveling — typically up to 10% of your total coverage amount (e.g., $2,500 on a $25,000 policy).

### 2. Liability Protection
If someone is injured in your apartment — or if you accidentally damage a neighbor's property — your liability coverage pays for:
- Legal defense costs
- Settlement or court-ordered damages
- Medical bills for injured guests (**no-fault medical coverage**, usually $1,000–$5,000)

Recommended minimum liability coverage: **$300,000**.

### 3. Additional Living Expenses (ALE)
If your apartment becomes unlivable due to a covered event (fire, severe water damage), ALE coverage pays the difference between your normal rent and the cost of temporary housing while repairs are made — including hotel stays and restaurant meals.

---

## What Renters Insurance Does NOT Cover

| Not Covered | What to Do Instead |
|---|---|
| Floods | Buy flood insurance through the National Flood Insurance Program (floodsmart.gov) |
| Earthquakes | Buy a separate earthquake policy or endorsement |
| Your roommate's belongings | Each roommate needs their own policy |
| Your car | Requires separate auto insurance |
| Intentional damage | Not covered under any policy |
| Very high-value items (jewelry, cameras) | Ask about a **floater** or endorsement to increase limits |

---

## Policy Types: Actual Cash Value vs. Replacement Cost

| Policy Type | How It Works | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Actual Cash Value** | Pays replacement cost minus depreciation (e.g., a 3-year-old laptop gets less than its original price) | Lower premiums, OK for older items |
| **Replacement Cost** | Pays the full cost to buy a new equivalent item, no depreciation deducted | Recommended for most renters — about 10% more expensive but worth it |

## How Much Does It Cost?

- **Typical cost:** $10–$20 per month
- **Starting cost:** As low as $5 per month for basic coverage
- Annual premium is usually $100–$240

### Ways to Save Money
- **Bundle** with auto insurance from the same company (often 5–15% discount)
- Install **smoke detectors, deadbolt locks, or a security system**
- Choose a **higher deductible** — raising your deductible from $500 to $1,000 can reduce your premium by up to 25%
- Non-smoker discounts may apply

---

## How to Buy Renters Insurance

1. **Get at least 3 quotes** from different companies — prices vary significantly
2. Compare through direct agents, independent brokers, and online sellers
3. Check the company's reputation for handling claims fairly
4. Ask friends or classmates for recommendations

**Good options for international students:**
- **Lemonade** — fully digital, sign up online in minutes, no phone call required, monthly billing available
- Major insurers: State Farm, Allstate, GEICO, Progressive

💡 You do not need a Social Security Number to buy renters insurance. A passport is typically sufficient ID.

## Special Notes for Students

- **Living in a dorm?** You may be covered under your parents' homeowners or renters insurance policy — but this is less relevant for international students whose parents' policies are in another country.
- **Living off-campus?** You almost certainly need your own separate renters insurance policy.
- **Roommates:** Your policy does **not** cover your roommates' belongings. Each person should have their own policy.
- **Valuable items:** Standard policies limit coverage for jewelry to about $1,500. If you own expensive jewelry, cameras, or musical instruments, ask about a **floater endorsement** to increase coverage for those items.

---

## How to File a Claim

If something happens, follow these steps:

1. **Report crimes to police first** — get a police report number and officer's name
2. **Contact your insurer immediately** — clarify what is covered and your deductible
3. **Document your losses** — photographs, receipts, and a detailed list of damaged or stolen items
4. **Do not throw away damaged items** — keep them until an insurance adjuster has inspected them
5. **Save all receipts** for temporary housing or other emergency expenses
6. **Submit your claim forms promptly** — delays can complicate your claim

If you are unhappy with how your insurer handles your claim, you can escalate to your state's **Department of Insurance**.
