# Security Deposit — How to Protect Your Money

A **security deposit** is money you pay your landlord before moving in. The landlord holds it during your tenancy and uses it to cover unpaid rent or damage beyond normal wear. After you move out, they must return the remainder to you — often within 14–30 days, depending on your state.

> 💡 Your security deposit is **legally your money**. The landlord is holding it in trust. You are entitled to get it back unless there is a valid reason for a deduction.

---

## How Much Can a Landlord Charge?

State law limits how large a security deposit can be. Common limits:

| State | Maximum Deposit |
|---|---|
| California | 2 months' rent (unfurnished) |
| New York | 1 month's rent (post-2019 law) |
| Connecticut | 2 months' rent (1 month for tenants age 62+) |
| Texas | No statutory limit (market rate) |
| Illinois / Chicago | Varies; Chicago requires landlords to pay interest on deposits |

⚠️ If your landlord charges more than the state limit, that is illegal. Contact a local tenant rights organization.

---

## What Can Be Deducted From Your Deposit?

### ✅ Landlord MAY deduct for:
- Unpaid rent
- Damage **beyond normal wear and tear** (see below)
- Excessive filth requiring professional cleaning
- Missing keys, fobs, or parking passes

### ❌ Landlord may NOT deduct for (Normal Wear and Tear):
- Faded paint from sunlight
- Small nail or tack holes from hanging pictures
- Worn carpet from regular foot traffic (especially after 3–5 years of use)
- Minor scuffs on walls or floors
- Loose or broken blind strings

### 🔴 Examples of Damage (Deductible):
- Large holes in walls
- Carpet stains, burn marks, or pet odors
- Broken windows or door locks
- Excessively scratched hardwood floors
- Appliances damaged by misuse

💡 **Carpet note:** Carpet has a useful life of about 3–5 years. If carpet is already old when you move in, you should not be charged for full replacement when you leave.

## How to Protect Your Deposit: The Most Important Steps

### Step 1 — Do a Move-In Inspection
Walk through every room **before you bring your belongings in**. Document every existing defect: scratches, stains, marks, broken fixtures. See the full process at [[guides/move-in]].

### Step 2 — Photograph Everything
Take clear photos and videos of every room, every defect, every wall, floor, and appliance. Date-stamp your photos by emailing them to yourself or your landlord on Day 1.

### Step 3 — Get the Inspection Report Signed
Both you and the landlord should sign the move-in condition checklist. **Keep your copy for the entire tenancy.**

### Step 4 — Email Everything to Your Landlord
Send photos and the signed checklist to your landlord by email on move-in day. This creates a **timestamped paper trail** that is very difficult to dispute later.

### Step 5 — Document Move-Out the Same Way
On move-out day, take a full walkthrough video. Request a joint walkthrough inspection with your landlord. Compare the move-out condition with your move-in photos. See [[guides/move-out]] for the full move-out process.

## When Does the Landlord Return the Deposit?

Most states require the landlord to:
1. Return the deposit (or what remains) within **14–30 days** after move-out
2. Provide a **written itemized list** of any deductions

If the landlord does not follow these rules, they may forfeit their right to keep any of the deposit.

---

## What to Do If Your Deposit Is Wrongfully Withheld

If your landlord does not return your deposit or provides deductions you believe are unfair:

1. **Send a written demand letter** via email and certified mail. State the amount owed and give a deadline (e.g., 14 days).
2. **Gather your evidence:** move-in photos, signed inspection report, move-out photos, lease agreement, all email records.
3. **File in small claims court** if the landlord does not respond. Small claims court does not require a lawyer. Filing fees are usually $30–$75. Limits vary by state ($5,000–$20,000).
4. **Penalties:** In states like California, a landlord who wrongfully withholds a deposit may owe you **2–3 times** the deposit amount as a penalty.

💡 Many university international student offices and legal aid clinics can help you write a demand letter for free.

---

## State-Specific Protections

Some states offer stronger protections than others:
- **Washington D.C., Alaska, Illinois, Wisconsin:** Stronger tenant protections
- **California:** 2–3× deposit penalty for wrongful withholding
- **Chicago, IL:** Landlord must pay interest on deposits held
- **Indiana, Michigan:** Weaker protections — documentation is especially important in these states

Always check your specific state and city laws. Your university's international student office or a local tenant rights hotline can help.
