# Communicating with Your Landlord

Good communication with your landlord protects you legally and helps you get problems resolved quickly. The most important rule is simple: **always get it in writing.**

---

## The Golden Rule: Everything in Writing

Written records are the foundation of resolving landlord-tenant disputes. A verbal agreement — a phone call, a conversation in the hallway — is almost impossible to prove later. A written record with a timestamp is very difficult to dispute.

**For every verbal conversation with your landlord, follow up immediately with an email summary:**

> *"Dear [Name], I am writing to confirm our phone conversation today. You mentioned that the heater repair will be completed by Friday, November 15. Please let me know if this timeline changes. Thank you."*

This simple habit protects you in every situation — from maintenance repairs to lease renewals to move-out inspections.

---

## Communication Methods

### 📧 Email — Best for Most Situations
Email is the preferred method for nearly all landlord communication.

**Use email for:**
- Maintenance requests
- Lease questions
- Move-out notice (also send by certified mail — see below)
- Disputing a charge
- Following up on unresolved issues
- Confirming any verbal conversation

**Tips:**
- Use a professional tone — clear, polite, and factual
- Use your university email address if possible (this adds credibility)
- Keep all emails sent and received in a dedicated folder (e.g., "Apartment — [Address]")
- Include your name, unit number, and address in every message

### 🖥️ Tenant Portal — If Your Building Offers One
Many large apartment complexes use online portals (common examples: RentCafe, AppFolio, Buildium) for rent payment and maintenance requests.

- Log all maintenance requests through the portal for a built-in record
- **Also follow up by email** after submitting a portal request — this gives you a copy outside the portal system that you control
- Take screenshots of submitted requests as additional backup

### 📮 Certified Mail — For Formal or Legal Notices
Use certified mail (with return receipt requested) when the stakes are high. This gives you proof of delivery — the post office provides a receipt showing that your letter was delivered and signed for.

**Use certified mail for:**
- Move-out notice
- Formal repair demands
- Security deposit disputes
- Any communication where you need legal proof of delivery

Certified mail is available at any U.S. Post Office for approximately $3–$5. Keep your receipt.

💡 Under Texas law (and similar laws in many states): if you send a repair request by certified mail, you only need to send **one** notice. If you send by regular mail or email only, send **two** notices to be safe.

### 📞 Phone or In-Person — For Urgent Issues
Phone calls and in-person conversations are fine for urgent situations or building rapport. However:

⚠️ **Always follow up every phone or in-person conversation with a written summary email.** Do this the same day. Include what was discussed and what was agreed upon.

## Professional Tone and Language

You do not need to be a lawyer to write effective landlord communications. Keep your emails:

- **Clear:** Say exactly what the problem is or what you need
- **Factual:** Include dates, amounts, and specific details
- **Polite but firm:** You have legal rights — state them calmly
- **Brief:** Get to the point quickly; do not include emotional language

**Avoid:**
- Threatening language or insults — this weakens your position
- Vague language — be specific about dates, issues, and what you are asking for
- Only sending one method — for important issues, use email AND certified mail

---

## What to Keep on File

Create a dedicated folder (physical or digital) for all apartment-related records:

- [ ] All emails sent and received — never delete apartment emails
- [ ] All maintenance requests and responses
- [ ] Move-in condition report and photos (signed copy)
- [ ] Move-out condition report and photos
- [ ] Rent payment receipts or bank statements showing payments
- [ ] Your signed lease agreement (keep a scanned copy in the cloud)
- [ ] Any notices received from the landlord (entry notices, rent increases, etc.)
- [ ] Certified mail receipts

💡 Use a free cloud storage service (Google Drive, Dropbox, iCloud) to back up all documents. If you move, you want access to these records even if your computer is lost.

---

## When Landlords Fail to Respond

If your landlord does not respond to a reasonable request:

1. Send a written follow-up referencing your original request and the date sent
2. Give a clear deadline: *"Please respond by [date]"*
3. Escalate to certified mail if the issue is serious
4. Document your attempts carefully — this evidence is important if you need to take legal action

## Email Checklist Before You Send

Before sending any important email to your landlord, confirm:

- [ ] Is your subject line specific? (e.g., "Maintenance Request — Unit 4B — Broken Heater")
- [ ] Did you include your name and unit number?
- [ ] Did you include the date and specific details of the issue?
- [ ] Did you attach any relevant photos?
- [ ] Is the tone professional and factual — not emotional?
- [ ] Did you state clearly what you are asking the landlord to do?
- [ ] Did you BCC yourself or save a copy?
