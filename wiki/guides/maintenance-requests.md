# How to Submit Maintenance Requests

As a tenant in the U.S., you have a **legal right to a habitable apartment**. Your landlord is required by law to make necessary repairs. This guide explains what that means, how to report problems effectively, and what to do if your landlord does not respond.

---

## Your Legal Right to Repairs

Most U.S. states recognize the **implied warranty of habitability** — a legal promise that your landlord makes simply by renting you an apartment: the property must be safe and livable. This warranty cannot be removed by the lease; it exists whether or not it is written down.

Landlords must maintain:
- **Heat and hot water** (water must reach at least 120°F)
- **Functioning smoke alarms** and compliance with fire safety codes
- **Structural integrity:** No major water leaks, dangerous cracks, or peeling paint with lead hazards
- **Pest-free conditions:** Landlords must address infestations of roaches, rodents, and other pests
- **Working plumbing:** Sewage backup is a health hazard and must be addressed urgently

For repairs to be the landlord's responsibility:
- Your rent must be current at the time you submit the notice
- The problem must be reported **in writing** to your landlord
- The issue must affect health or safety (or involve utilities like heat or hot water)

## Categories of Urgency

### 🚨 Emergency — Contact Your Landlord Immediately (Day or Night)
These situations require **immediate action**. Do not wait until business hours.

- Gas leak — **call 911 and your gas company first, leave the building immediately**
- Major water leak or flooding
- Complete loss of electricity
- No heat in winter
- Sewage backup
- Broken door or window lock (security risk)
- Fire — call 911 first

### ⚠️ Urgent — Report Within 24 Hours
- Refrigerator not working (food will spoil)
- Stove or oven not working
- Air conditioning failure during extreme heat
- Only toilet in the unit is clogged

### 📋 Routine — Report Within a Few Days
- Dripping faucet
- Broken cabinet door or drawer
- Burned-out light fixture
- Minor pest sighting (one or two insects)
- Broken window blind

## How to Submit a Repair Request

### Step 1 — Take Photos or Video
Document the problem clearly before you report it. Photograph every angle of the defect and note where it is in the apartment and when it started.

### Step 2 — Submit in Writing
Always use **written communication** — not verbal requests alone. Options:
- **Email** (preferred — creates a clear timestamped record)
- **Tenant portal** (many large apartment buildings use systems like RentCafe, AppFolio, or Buildium) — use the portal AND send a follow-up email

**For serious issues (heat, major leaks):** Also send written notice by **certified mail, return receipt requested**. Under Texas law (and similar rules in many states), if you send certified mail, you only need to send one written notice. Otherwise, send two written notices.

Use the ready-to-copy template at [[templates/maintenance-email]].

### Step 3 — Be Specific
Include in your request:
- Your name, unit number, and building address
- What the problem is and where exactly it is located
- When you first noticed the problem
- Whether it poses a safety risk
- Your availability for maintenance staff to enter

### Step 4 — Allow Reasonable Time
Landlords are given a reasonable period to make repairs:
- **Minimum 7 days** for routine issues
- **Less time** for genuine emergencies (heat, leaks, safety hazards)

### Step 5 — Follow Up in Writing
If there is no response after the deadline, send a written follow-up. See [[templates/maintenance-email]] for a follow-up template.

## Protection from Retaliation

Your landlord **cannot punish you** for submitting legitimate repair requests. Retaliation is illegal. Prohibited retaliatory actions include:
- Evicting you
- Terminating your lease
- Raising your rent (unless it was a scheduled, building-wide increase)

**Retaliation protections last 6 months** after your repair request in Texas (and similar protections exist in most states). Document all communications carefully — see [[guides/landlord-communication]].

## What to Do If Repairs Are Not Made

If your landlord ignores written repair requests:

| Option | What It Involves |
|---|---|
| **Terminate the lease** | Give written notice, receive refund for unused rental period, and recover your deposit minus lawful deductions |
| **Withhold rent or repair-and-deduct** | ⚠️ Varies significantly by state — do NOT do this without legal guidance |
| **Sue in small claims court** | No lawyer required; courts typically hear cases within 10 days; you may recover one month's rent plus $500 (minimum in Texas), court costs, and attorney's fees |
| **Report code violations** | Contact your city or county housing authority; landlords cannot legally retaliate for this |

## Documentation Best Practices

- **Always use writing** — email or tenant portal, not just verbal requests
- **Attach photos or video** of the problem with every submission
- **Note the date** the problem started and exactly where it is in the unit
- **State your availability** for maintenance staff to enter
- **Save every email and response** — create a dedicated email folder for all landlord communications
