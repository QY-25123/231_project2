# Apartment Parking Rules in the U.S.

Parking at a U.S. apartment complex can be confusing, especially if you are new to driving in America or come from a country where parking customs differ. This guide explains the most common parking arrangements, rules, and what to do if your car is towed.

---

## Types of Parking Arrangements

### Assigned Parking
Your lease gives you a **specific numbered parking spot** that only you may use.

- Parking in another resident's assigned spot is one of the **most common reasons cars are towed** at apartment complexes
- Some buildings charge extra monthly fees for covered or garage parking ($50–$200/month)
- Check your lease to confirm whether parking is included in your rent or billed separately

### Permit Parking
Your building or your city issues a **parking permit** — a sticker, hang tag, or digital pass — that must be displayed in your vehicle at all times.

- **Building permits:** Required to park in the complex's lot or garage
- **City-issued residential permits:** Required in dense neighborhoods in cities like Washington D.C., Boston, Chicago, and San Francisco — these are separate from any permit your building issues
- Get your permit **on or before move-in day** — a missing permit is a towing trigger

### Visitor Parking
Most apartment complexes have designated visitor spots, usually with **time limits** (2–24 hours).

- Some buildings require visitors to register with the management office for a temporary pass
- Overstaying visitor parking time limits is a **frequent towing trigger**
- Do not use visitor spots for your own vehicle, even temporarily

### Street Parking
If you park on the street, you must follow **posted signs carefully**. Common restrictions include:

- **Street cleaning days:** Signs like "No Parking 8am–10am Mon & Thu" — your car will be ticketed or towed
- **Overnight parking restrictions:** Some cities prohibit overnight street parking without a permit
- **Residential permit zones:** Only vehicles with a city-issued permit may park in these zones

Fines for street parking violations are typically **$50–$150** per ticket.

## Common Reasons Cars Are Towed

Be aware of these towing triggers:

- 🔴 Parking in a **fire lane** (red curb) or blocking a **fire hydrant** (yellow curb)
- 🔴 Parking in **another resident's assigned spot**
- 🔴 **Missing or expired** parking permit
- 🔴 Blocking a **driveway or accessible (handicapped) parking space**
- 🔴 Overstaying **visitor parking** time limits
- 🔴 Abandoned or **inoperable vehicles**

---

## If Your Car Is Towed

Act quickly — storage fees accumulate every day.

1. **Call building management** to find out which towing company they use
2. **Contact the towing company** to confirm your vehicle's location
3. **Go in person** with: cash (many towing companies do not accept cards), your driver's license, and your vehicle registration
4. **Retrieval fees:** Typically $150–$350 plus daily storage fees of $30–$60/day
5. If you believe the tow was **unauthorized**, document everything in writing and contact local law enforcement

⚠️ If you cannot afford the retrieval fee right away, call the towing company — some offer payment arrangements, but storage fees continue to accrue.

## Common Lease Parking Rules

Most apartment leases prohibit:
- Vehicle repairs in the parking lot (oil changes, tire changes, etc.)
- Storing inoperable or unregistered vehicles
- Washing your car in the parking lot
- Boats, RVs, campers, or trailers (without specific written permission)

Violating parking rules in your lease can result in your vehicle being towed and, in serious or repeated cases, lease penalties.

## For International Students with a Vehicle

### Driver's License
- Your **home country driver's license** is often valid in the U.S. for the **first year** of your stay, but this varies by state
- **California** requires you to obtain a California driver's license within **10 days** of establishing residency
- Check your specific state's Department of Motor Vehicles (DMV) website for the rules

### Vehicle Registration
- If you buy or bring a car, register it in your new state within **30–90 days** of arrival (varies by state)
- You will need your title, proof of insurance, and ID

### Insurance
- **U.S. law requires liability insurance** for all vehicles
- Always keep your insurance card and vehicle registration in the car
- If you are stopped by police without insurance, penalties are severe

### No Car? Ask About Bicycle Storage
Many apartment complexes have **secured bicycle rooms** — a great option if you do not own a car. Ask when touring apartments.

## Questions to Ask Before Signing a Lease

- Is parking included in my rent, or is it an additional monthly fee?
- How many spots does my unit include?
- Is there designated visitor parking, and what is the time limit?
- Where and how do I get my parking permit?
- Are there restrictions on vehicle types (RVs, trucks, motorcycles)?
- Which towing company does the building use?
