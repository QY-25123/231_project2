# Setting Up Utilities in a U.S. Apartment

**Utilities** are the essential services that make your apartment livable — electricity, gas, water, and internet. Some may be included in your rent; others you must set up and pay for yourself. This guide explains what to expect and how to get everything working before you move in.

---

## First: Read Your Lease

Your lease should specify which utilities you are responsible for and which the landlord covers. Common arrangements:

- **All utilities included:** One flat monthly rent covers everything (more common in furnished apartments)
- **Some included:** Water and trash included; electricity, gas, and internet are your responsibility
- **None included:** You pay all utilities separately

⚠️ **Always get a written utility disclosure from your landlord before signing.** Ask for a list of which utilities you pay, which are included, and how charges are split if there are shared meters.

For questions about what is in your lease, see [[concepts/lease-terms]].

## Types of Utilities

### ⚡ Electricity
- Powers your lights, outlets, appliances, and often heating and cooling
- Provided by your local electric utility company (examples: PG&E in the San Francisco Bay Area, Con Edison in New York City, ComEd in Chicago)
- Contact the utility company directly to set up an account in your name

### 🔥 Natural Gas
- Powers gas stoves, water heaters, and furnaces in many apartments
- In some cities, the same company provides both electricity and gas; in others, they are separate
- Usage increases significantly in winter if you use gas heating

### 💧 Water and Sewer
- Often included in apartment rent
- If not included, typically billed quarterly
- Contact your landlord to confirm whether you need to set up a water account

### 🌐 Internet
- Almost never included in rent
- Providers vary by region: Comcast/Xfinity, AT&T, Spectrum, Google Fiber, and others
- Allow **1–2 weeks** for an installation appointment — schedule early
- Watch for **promotional rates** that expire after 12 months — your bill may jump significantly in year two

### 🗑️ Trash and Recycling
- Usually handled by the building and included in rent
- Ask your landlord where the trash room or dumpster is and what the pickup schedule is
- Learn your building's recycling rules — many U.S. cities have mandatory recycling requirements

## Step-by-Step Setup

Follow these steps to get your utilities running smoothly:

1. **Read your lease** — identify exactly which utilities you are responsible for
2. **Ask your landlord** for the names of the providers they use for the building
3. **Contact providers at least 1–2 weeks before your move-in date** to start service in your name on or before move-in day
4. **What you will need when you call or go online:**
   - Your full name
   - Your new apartment address and unit number
   - Your move-in date
   - A form of ID (passport is accepted by most providers)
5. **Security deposits for utilities:** If you have no U.S. credit history, some providers may require a deposit of $150–$250. This is typically refundable after 12 months of on-time payments.
6. **Set up autopay** from your bank account to avoid late fees and service interruptions

## Your Legal Right to Essential Utilities

Under the **implied warranty of habitability**, your landlord must ensure that essential services — including heat, hot water, and electricity — remain available. If your landlord controls a utility and fails to provide it:

- You may have the right to withhold rent, pay for the service yourself, or terminate your lease
- You can report code violations without fear of retaliation
- Contact your local tenant rights organization or your state's housing authority

See [[guides/maintenance-requests]] if a utility in your building stops working.

## For International Students

- **No Social Security Number (SSN)?** Most utility companies will open an account with a passport. Some may accept your I-20 or I-94 as supporting documentation.
- **Sharing costs with roommates?** Use apps like **Splitwise** to split bills fairly and track who has paid.
- **Cold-climate cities:** If you are moving to Chicago, Boston, Minneapolis, or similar cities, winter heating bills can be **much higher** than summer bills. Plan your budget accordingly.
- **Budget billing:** Many utility companies offer a program that averages your costs across 12 months so you pay a consistent amount every month instead of a large bill in winter.

## Estimated Monthly Costs

These are approximate figures for a one-bedroom apartment. Costs vary significantly by city, climate, and usage.

| Utility | Typical Monthly Range |
|---|---|
| Electricity | $40 – $120 |
| Natural Gas | $20 – $80 |
| Water (if not included) | $20 – $50 |
| Internet | $40 – $80 |
| **Total** | **$120 – $330** |

💡 These costs are in addition to your monthly rent. When budgeting, add $120–$330 to your monthly rent figure to get a realistic total housing cost.
